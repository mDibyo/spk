Munch India

3015 Shattuck Ave., Berkeley
Janelle Bitker / The Chronicle

In 2011, chefs Diana Afroza and Nick Ahmed began roving around the Bay Area in their truck, selling regional dishes from India, Bangladesh and Pakistan interpreted through the chefs’ own points of view: Ingredients like Japanese yuzu, seasonal California produce and sustainable meat and fish made appearances in their dishes. When Afroza and Ahmed transitioned the concept to a brick-and-mortar in 2018 in Berkeley, just steps from Berkeley Bowl, they preserved their particular brand of rigor. Here, Indo-Chinese noodles ($14.99) get their smoky wok hei aroma from a bona fide wok, and kebabs are carefully grilled over hardwood and lump charcoal. While the restaurant began as an upscale dinner destination, the pandemic made it transition into a more casual takeout-and-delivery-only spot, with a menu that changes monthly.

Pakwan

501 O’Farrell St., San Francisco
Yalonda M. James / The Chronicle
Yalonda M. James / The Chronicle
Yalonda M. James / The Chronicle

In 1999, ​​Khalid Amin and Mohammad Shahbaz opened Pakwan in the Tenderloin, charming San Franciscans with their affordable menu of halal tandoori dishes and curries. A runaway hit, the restaurant has since expanded to a five-location group, with spots in Hayward and Fremont as well. Accessible prices mean this is the best place to order a huge spread: chickpea masala ($9.99) garnished with thick slices of chile pepper; platters of smoky chicken tandoori and kebabs; and the ever-popular chicken tikka masala ($13.99) with lots of naan on the side. The restaurant successfully straddles the line between traditional and crowd-pleasing, making it popular with newbies and old hands alike.

Punjabi Kitchen

3550 San Pablo Dam Rd., El Sobrante

Finding Punjabi Kitchen feels akin to stepping into a wardrobe and seeing Narnia: You have to know about it or you’ll miss it entirely. Tucked away in the back of Bottles, an Indian grocery and beer store in El Sobrante, the restaurant and craft beer taproom, run by Daljit Uppal and his family, is as discreet as a clubhouse. When the dining room opens for dinner, customers can order flights of craft beer to sip on while digging into vegetable biryani ($11.99), delicate parathas stuffed with steamy chunks of potato and cauliflower ($4.99), and Navaratan korma ($12.99), a creamy vegetable dish with nuts and cheese. For those wanting more typical fare with their ale, the store also carries Krispy Krunchy Chicken.

Puranpoli

3074B Scott Blvd., Santa Clara
Carolyn Fong / Special To The Chronicle
Carolyn Fong / Special To The Chronicle

Some of India’s most famous snack foods come from Maharashtra, including pav bhaji, those delectable buttered buns served with piquant vegetable gravy, and vada pav, a highly craveable potato patty sandwich. Puranpoli, a Santa Clara vegetarian restaurant run by a team of siblings, has all that plus less-known specialties like zunka ($13.99), a comforting dish of chickpea flour paste sauteed with lots of aromatics and curry leaves. Each entree can be made into a “combo” with fresh chapatis ($1.50), fries ($3.50) and other carbs.

Wah Jee Wah

32681 Mission Blvd., Hayward
Don Feria / Special To The Chronicle
Don Feria / Special To The Chronicle
Don Feria / Special To The Chronicle

After overcoming a life-threatening health issue, chef Rounak Dumra returned to his restaurant Wah Jee Wah stronger than ever and with a renewed focus that shows up on the plate. The restaurant’s expertise lies in charcoal-grilled skewers, which you can try in a combo plate ($24), or half Big Boy Platter ($60) to feed many. The juicy chicken legs and lamb seek are definitely worthwhile, but give the bouncy mushrooms and grilled paneer a try, too. The menu is stacked with smaller, fun plates like roasted peanuts over a crisp, chickpea wafer papadum ($6), along with masala mac and cheese ($8). Chef Durma has a carousel of specials on offer, the most recent of which is a luxuriously creamy, acidic butter chicken ($14).

Zareen’s

365 California Ave., Palo Alto

Since Zareen and Umair Khan opened their first location in Mountain View in 2014, Zareen’s has been a popular spot for diners on the Peninsula seeking homestyle South Asian dishes and a steamy cup of chai. Cumin-scented beef chapli kebabs, normally served alone or with a warm naan on the streets of South Asia, get the burger treatment here with a pillowy bun and a bright green swipe of mint chutney ($11.99). A side of fries is dusted with warm masala spices. The samosa burger ($8.75) is also an excellent vegetarian sandwich option.
